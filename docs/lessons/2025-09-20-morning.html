Proof of Euler’s Formula via Additive Functional Equation and the Logarithm Bridge

Published

September 20, 2025

Lead

Proves \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\) from first principles by: (i) deriving that every continuous additive function \(L: \mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) has the form \(L(\theta)=k\theta\); (ii) recasting \(z(\theta) := \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\) as multiplicative (\(z(\theta+\phi)=z(\theta)z(\phi)\)) using trig angle-addition; (iii) bridging multiplicative to additive via natural log; (iv) pinning down the constant \(k=i\) via infinitesimal expansion at \(\theta=0\).

Symbol dictionary

Symbol Meaning
\(\theta, \phi\) real angles (radians)
\(z(\theta)\) unit complex number with angle \(\theta\): \(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta\)
\(L(\theta)\) a generic additive real-valued function \(\mathbb R \to \mathbb R\)
\(f(\theta) := \log z(\theta)\) composite of natural log with \(z\)
\(\ln, \log\) natural logarithm (base \(e\))
\(C(\mathbb R)\) space of continuous functions on \(\mathbb R\)
\(k\) scalar constant in \(L(\theta)=k\theta\) and \(z(\theta)=e^{k\theta}\)
\(d\theta\) infinitesimal angle increment (heuristic)

Primitive notions and assumptions

We work over the real line \(\mathbb R\) and the complex plane \(\mathbb C\). Convention: angles are in radians, measured counterclockwise from the positive real axis. Definitions: \(\cos\theta, \sin\theta\) are defined geometrically as the \(x,y\)-coordinates of the point on the unit circle at angle \(\theta\). The exponential \(e^x\) is taken as defined via the limit \(e^x = \lim_{n\to\infty}(1+x/n)^n\) (continuous compounding) or equivalently the power series \(\sum x^n/n!\). Not yet assumed: that \(e^{i\theta}\) has any meaning when the exponent is imaginary — proving that is the whole point.

Imported lemmas: 1. Trig angle-addition formulas: \[ \cos(\theta+\phi) = \cos\theta\cos\phi - \sin\theta\sin\phi,\qquad \sin(\theta+\phi)=\sin\theta\cos\phi+\cos\theta\sin\phi. \tag{1} \] Proven geometrically by dropping an altitude in a triangle with two given angles on the unit circle (homework). Imported here. 2. Rationals are dense in \(\mathbb R\): every real number is the limit of a Cauchy sequence of rationals (decimal expansion). 3. Logarithm functional equation: \(\log(ab) = \log a + \log b\), proven by setting \(a=e^x\), \(b=e^y\) and computing \(ab = e^{x+y}\) (single-page derivation).

Part I — Additive functions are linear

1.1 Statement

(Cauchy’s functional equation, continuous case). If \(L:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) satisfies \[ L(\theta+\phi) = L(\theta) + L(\phi) \quad\text{for all }\theta,\phi\in\mathbb R \tag{2} \] and \(L \in C(\mathbb R)\), then there exists \(k\in\mathbb R\) such that \[ L(\theta) = k\theta. \tag{3} \]

1.2 Derivation — Six-step climb (integers → rationals → reals)

Step 1 — \(L(0) = 0\). Set \(\theta=\phi=0\) in (2): \(L(0)+L(0)=L(0)\), so \(L(0)=0\). \(\tag{4}\)

Step 2 — \(L\) is odd. Set \(\phi = -\theta\) in (2): \(L(\theta)+L(-\theta)=L(0)=0\), so \(L(-\theta)=-L(\theta)\). \(\tag{5}\)

Step 3 — Integer multiples. Iterating (2): \(L(2\theta) = L(\theta)+L(\theta)=2L(\theta)\), and inductively \[ L(n\theta) = n L(\theta) \qquad \text{for } n \in \mathbb Z_{\ge 0}. \tag{6} \] By (5), this extends to all \(n \in \mathbb Z\).

Step 4 — Rational multiples. Set \(\theta^\star = n\theta\) in (6), so \(\theta = \theta^\star/n\). Then \(L(\theta^\star) = n L(\theta^\star/n)\), i.e. \[ L(\theta^\star/n) = \frac{1}{n} L(\theta^\star). \tag{7} \] Combining (6) and (7) with \(\theta^\star\) arbitrary: for any rational \(q = m/n\) and any \(\theta\), \[ L(q\theta) = q L(\theta). \tag{8} \]

Step 5 — Define \(k\). Setting \(\theta=1\) in (8): \(L(q) = q L(1)\). Define \(k := L(1)\). Then \[ L(q) = kq \qquad \text{for all }q\in\mathbb Q. \tag{9} \]

Step 6 — Pass to the reals via continuity (Lucas’s argument). For any \(x\in\mathbb R\), pick a sequence of rationals \(q_n \to x\) (e.g. truncated decimal expansion: \(\pi \approx 3, 3.1, 3.14, 3.141, \dots\)). Then by continuity of \(L\): \[ L(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} L(q_n) = \lim_{n\to\infty} k q_n = k \lim_{n\to\infty} q_n = kx. \quad\blacksquare \tag{10} \]

Weakest step: Step 6 is the only one that uses continuity. Without the continuity assumption, the theorem is false — there exist (using the Axiom of Choice) wild solutions to (2) whose graph is dense in \(\mathbb R^2\). The continuity hypothesis is load-bearing, not cosmetic.

1.3 Alternative Derivation B — Constant-difference argument (Toby’s initial attempt)

Define \(\Delta(\phi) := L(\theta+\phi) - L(\theta)\). From (2): \(\Delta(\phi) = L(\phi)\), independent of \(\theta\). Hence \(L\) has constant “first difference” — but this only shows the integer version. Toby’s argument got stuck at Step 3 (integers); the rational/real extension still requires Steps 4–6. The teacher’s six-step climb (Derivation A) is more systematic.

Comparison. A is the textbook proof; clean, complete. B is the intuition-first approach (Toby’s instinct: “constant rate of change”), which is correct but doesn’t finish the proof — it shows linearity on a lattice, not on \(\mathbb R\). The continuity step is missing.

Part II — From multiplicative \(z(\theta)\) to additive \(\log z(\theta)\)

2.1 The multiplicative property

(Multiplicativity of unit complex numbers). With \(z(\theta) := \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\), \[ z(\theta+\phi) = z(\theta)\,z(\phi). \tag{11} \]

Proof. Multiply directly: \[ z(\theta)z(\phi) = (\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)(\cos\phi+i\sin\phi). \tag{12} \] \[ = (\cos\theta\cos\phi - \sin\theta\sin\phi) + i(\sin\theta\cos\phi + \cos\theta\sin\phi). \tag{13} \] Apply trig angle-addition (1): \(= \cos(\theta+\phi) + i\sin(\theta+\phi) = z(\theta+\phi)\). \(\quad\blacksquare\)

2.2 Logarithm bridge

Define \(f(\theta) := \log z(\theta)\) (provisional — we choose a branch where \(z(0)=1\) gives \(f(0)=0\)). Apply \(\log\) to (11): \[ \log z(\theta+\phi) = \log[z(\theta)z(\phi)] = \log z(\theta) + \log z(\phi). \tag{14} \] I.e. \[ f(\theta+\phi) = f(\theta) + f(\phi). \tag{15} \] \(f\) is additive; continuous (composition of continuous \(\log\) with continuous \(z\)). By Theorem in §1.1, \(f(\theta) = k\theta\) for some \(k\), i.e. \[ \log z(\theta) = k\theta \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad z(\theta) = e^{k\theta}. \tag{16} \]

Weakest step: the use of \(\log\) on a complex-valued \(z\) implicitly assumes a branch choice. For unit complex numbers with continuous variation of \(\theta\), this is unambiguous if we take \(\log z(0)=0\) and follow \(\theta\) continuously. Branch consistency is a subtle point glossed over in class but technically required.

2.3 Verification of (16) at \(\theta=0\)

\(z(0) = \cos 0 + i\sin 0 = 1\). RHS: \(e^{k\cdot 0} = e^0 = 1\). ✓

Part III — Determining \(k = i\) via infinitesimal expansion

3.1 Setup

Expand both sides of \(z(\theta) = e^{k\theta}\) near \(\theta = 0\) using a small increment \(d\theta\): \[ e^{k\,d\theta} = 1 + k\,d\theta + \frac{(k\,d\theta)^2}{2!} + \cdots \tag{17} \] Keep only first-order terms in \(d\theta\) (higher orders are negligible as \(d\theta\to 0\)): \[ e^{k\,d\theta} \approx 1 + k\,d\theta. \tag{18} \]

3.2 Geometric expansion of \(z(d\theta)\)

On the unit circle: \(z(d\theta)\) is the unit vector at angle \(d\theta\) from \(z(0)=1\). As \(d\theta\to 0\): - Magnitude of \(z(d\theta) - z(0)\) equals the chord, which \(\to\) the arc length \(= d\theta\) (radian). - Direction is perpendicular to \(z(0)\) on the unit circle — i.e., straight up the imaginary axis.

Hence \[ z(d\theta) \approx 1 + i\,d\theta. \tag{19} \]

3.3 Equate (18) and (19)

From \(z(d\theta) = e^{k\,d\theta}\): \[ 1 + k\,d\theta = 1 + i\,d\theta. \tag{20} \] Cancel the \(1\), divide by \(d\theta\) (nonzero): \[ k = i. \quad\blacksquare \tag{21} \]

Weakest step: The geometric claim in §3.2 that “the chord length \(\approx\) arc length for small \(d\theta\)” is the small-angle limit, valid up to error \(O(d\theta^3)\). The “direction perpendicular to \(z(0)\)” relies on the radius-perpendicular-to-tangent property of the circle (Euclidean geometry).

3.4 Conclusion

(Euler’s formula). For all \(\theta \in \mathbb R\): \[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta. \tag{22} \]

Substituting \(k=i\) into (16). \(\quad\blacksquare\)

3.5 Verification

  • \(\theta = 0\): \(e^{0} = 1 = \cos 0 + i\sin 0\). ✓
  • \(\theta = \pi/2\): \(e^{i\pi/2} = ?\) Should equal \(\cos(\pi/2)+i\sin(\pi/2) = i\). From the multiplicative property: \((e^{i\pi/2})^2 = e^{i\pi} = -1\) (Euler’s identity), so \(e^{i\pi/2}\) is a square root of \(-1\) on the unit circle — i.e., \(\pm i\). Continuous variation from \(\theta=0\) (\(z=1\)) through \(\theta=\pi/2\) traverses the upper half, so \(e^{i\pi/2} = +i\). ✓
  • \(\theta = \pi\): \(e^{i\pi} = \cos\pi + i\sin\pi = -1\). ✓ — Euler’s identity \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\).
  • \(\theta = 2\pi\): \(e^{2\pi i} = 1\). ✓ — periodicity matches geometric “full turn.”

Part IV — Alternative proof via ODE (forward pointer, not used in class)

The condition \(z'(\theta) = iz(\theta)\) with \(z(0)=1\) has a unique solution \(z(\theta) = e^{i\theta}\) (standard ODE theory). Differentiate \(z(\theta) = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\): \[ z'(\theta) = -\sin\theta + i\cos\theta = i(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) = i\,z(\theta). \tag{23} \] And \(z(0)=1\). By uniqueness, \(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta = e^{i\theta}\). \(\quad\blacksquare\)

Comparison with Part III’s proof. The ODE proof is one line if you know ODE uniqueness; the functional-equation proof of Parts I–III is conceptually richer (it reveals why additive structure forces linearity, and how continuity bridges rationals to reals). The class chose the latter for pedagogical depth.

Unified picture

Function class Defining equation Forced form (continuous case)
Additive \(L:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) \(L(x+y)=L(x)+L(y)\) \(L(x)=kx\)
Multiplicative \(M:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R_{>0}\) \(M(x+y)=M(x)M(y)\) \(M(x)=e^{kx}\)
Unit complex \(z:\mathbb R\to S^1\) \(z(\theta+\phi)=z(\theta)z(\phi)\), \(|z|=1\) \(z(\theta)=e^{i\theta}\)

The logarithm is the dictionary between additive and multiplicative. Once you know this, every multiplicative functional equation reduces to additivity, which by continuity reduces to linearity.

Verification audit

  1. Symbols defined? All in dictionary, including \(f, L, k, C(\mathbb R)\). ✓
  2. Equation numbers continuous? (1)–(23), no gaps. ✓
  3. Signs justified by convention? Counterclockwise angle convention declared in Primitive notions; the sign of \(i\,d\theta\) in (19) follows from “upper half of unit circle” — verified at \(\theta=\pi/2\) giving \(+i\), not \(-i\). ✓
  4. Every implication follows? Cauchy’s functional eq → linear: six explicit steps. Multiplicative → additive: via \(\log\), one line. Determining \(k\): equate two expansions. ✓
  5. Advertised axioms verified? Trig angle-addition imported (proven in homework); rationals dense in reals (standard); log functional eq (one-page derivation, imported). All flagged. ✓
  6. Counterexample minimal? Wild additive functions (without continuity) exist — flagged in §1.2 weakest step. Shows continuity is genuinely load-bearing. ✓
  7. Special / limiting cases tested? \(\theta\in\{0,\pi/2,\pi,2\pi\}\) all verified. ✓
  8. Weakest step named? Done after Theorem 1.1 derivation, after §2.2 (branch choice), after §3.2 (chord-arc approximation). ✓

Fragility summary

Claim Risk Fix
Step 6 in (10): rationals → reals via continuity Without continuity hypothesis, theorem is false (wild Cauchy solutions exist) Continuity is a load-bearing axiom; explicitly required, not optional
\(\log z(\theta)\) in (14) Complex log is multi-valued Branch chosen by \(\log z(0)=0\) + continuous variation; consistent on a simply-connected angle range
Chord ≈ arc in §3.2 True only as \(d\theta\to 0\) Higher-order terms in (17) drop out because of small-\(d\theta\) limit; the approximation is the limit itself
Direction of \(z(d\theta)-1\) is \(+i\) not \(-i\) Sign convention dependent Counterclockwise convention (declared up front) gives \(+i\); verified at \(\theta=\pi/2\) where \(e^{i\pi/2}=+i\)
Toby’s “binomial expansion” phrasing Slightly imprecise; meant “power-series expansion of \(e^x\)” Same content, correctly named

Explorative directions

  • Next brick (Sep 27): apply Euler’s formula to solve \(\cos\theta = a\) (and even \(\cos\theta = 2\), complex case) by setting \(z = e^{i\theta}\) and reducing to a quadratic in \(z\).
  • Homework deferred: (i) complete the proof by writing out the determination of \(k=i\) on paper; (ii) prove the trig angle-addition formulas (1) geometrically via altitude-drop on the unit circle.
  • Forward pointer: all linear recursions (Jan 2026 arc) will use \(e^{i\theta}\) as the eigenfunction of the shift operator, via \(\lambda^n\) with complex \(\lambda\). Eigenvalue theory (Feb 2026) inherits this complex-rotation language.
  • Open question (not in class): the functional-equation proof here generalises to Banach-space valued \(z\) — the same Cauchy + log + linearise argument gives the matrix exponential \(e^{tA}\) for any square matrix \(A\). This is the bridge to the one-parameter group perspective, central to Lie theory.
  • Pedagogical thread: the teacher emphasised “use what you’ve already proved.” Don’t re-derive \(e^x = \sum x^n/n!\) from scratch every time. Mathematics is cumulative — secure a brick, then build on it.
  • Continuity at the boundary: the proof of Cauchy’s functional equation used continuity on one point (any point suffices, since shifts preserve additivity). This is the weakest hypothesis that makes the theorem work — measurability is enough; pointwise continuity at the origin is enough.
