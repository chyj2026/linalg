Roots of Unity Sum to Zero by Symmetry; Order Divides φ(n); Symmetries of Square and Cube

Published

September 13, 2025

Lead

Three independent proofs that \(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^k=0\) for the \(n\)-th roots of unity (\(n\ge 2\)): (i) geometric series identity, (ii) Holmes’s concatenation as a closed regular polygon, (iii) rotational-invariance argument \(R=\omega R \Rightarrow R=0\). Then proves the order of every element divides \(\varphi(n)\) via Euclidean-division contradiction. Counts the symmetries of the square (8 = \(D_4\)) and cube (48 = \(S_4 \times \mathbb Z_2\)). Application: last three digits of \(7^{2025}\) by mod 8 + mod 125 CRT.

Symbol dictionary

Symbol Meaning
\(\omega\) primitive \(n\)-th root of unity, \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}\)
\(\omega^k\) the \(k\)-th \(n\)-th root of unity, \(0\le k\le n-1\)
\(R\) the sum vector \(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^k\) in \(\mathbb C\)
\(\text{ord}(a)\) order of \(a\) in \((\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)^\times\): smallest \(d\ge 1\) with \(a^d\equiv 1\)
\(\varphi(n)\) Euler totient (defined Sep 13 morning)
\(D_n\) dihedral group of the regular \(n\)-gon, \(\lvert D_n\rvert = 2n\)
\(|G|\) order (cardinality) of group \(G\)
\(S_n\) symmetric group on \(n\) letters, \(|S_n|=n!\)

Primitive notions and assumptions

We work in the complex plane \(\mathbb C\) with the standard polar form. Convention: angles measured counterclockwise from positive real axis; \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}\) is the rotation by exactly one \(n\)-th of a full turn. Imported lemmas: (i) Euler’s formula \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\) (to be proven Sep 20 — used here as imported); (ii) the geometric series identity \(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} z^k = (z^n-1)/(z-1)\) for \(z\ne 1\) (algebraic; one-line proof by multiplying out); (iii) Fermat / Euler from Sep 13 morning.

Part I — Three proofs that \(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^k = 0\)

1.1 Statement

(Sum of \(n\)-th roots of unity). For \(n \ge 2\): \[ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^k \;=\; 0 \qquad \text{where } \omega = e^{2\pi i/n}. \tag{1} \]

1.2 Derivation A — Geometric series (one line)

\(\omega^n = e^{2\pi i} = 1\) by Euler’s formula, so \(\omega\) is a root of \(z^n - 1\). For \(\omega \ne 1\) (i.e. \(n\ge 2\)): \[ \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^k = \frac{\omega^n - 1}{\omega - 1} = \frac{0}{\omega - 1} = 0. \quad\blacksquare \tag{2} \]

Weakest step: the division in (2) is only valid because \(\omega \ne 1\); the case \(n=1\) (where \(\omega=1\) and the sum is trivially 1) is correctly excluded by the hypothesis \(n\ge 2\).

1.3 Derivation B — Rotational invariance (the symmetry argument)

Let \(R := \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^k \in \mathbb C\). Multiply both sides by \(\omega\): \[ \omega R = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\omega^{k+1} = \sum_{j=1}^{n}\omega^{j} = \omega^n + \sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\omega^j = 1 + \sum_{j=1}^{n-1}\omega^j. \tag{3} \] The right side of (3) is just \(\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\omega^j = R\) with indices shifted by one. Hence: \[ \omega R = R \quad\Longrightarrow\quad (\omega - 1) R = 0. \tag{4} \] Since \(\omega \ne 1\), \(R = 0\). \(\quad\blacksquare\)

Weakest step: the shift-of-indices step in (3) is the load-bearing move; it works because multiplication by \(\omega\) permutes the set \(\{1,\omega,\dots,\omega^{n-1}\}\) — exactly the geometric image of the multiplication-permutation argument from Sep 13 morning.

1.4 Derivation C — Concatenation as a closed polygon (Holmes’s argument)

Think of each \(\omega^k\) as a unit vector. Place them tip-to-tail. The angle between consecutive vectors (after concatenation) is constant: each next vector is the previous one rotated by \(2\pi/n\). After \(n\) steps, the cumulative rotation is \(2\pi\), returning to the original direction. The resulting path is a closed regular \(n\)-gon, hence the total displacement (= the sum) is zero. \(\quad\blacksquare\)

Weakest step: “the path closes” relies on the fact that turning by \(2\pi/n\) at each of \(n\) steps brings the heading back to where it started — true by elementary trigonometry, but the geometric closure of the path requires the side lengths to be uniform (they are, all = 1) and the turning angle constant. Both hold; the argument is rigorous but appeals to geometric intuition for the polygon-closure step.

1.5 Comparison

  • A is the shortest and most algebraic. Imports the geometric series identity (one-line lemma).
  • B is the most structurally illuminating — it makes the connection to group symmetry (`multiplication by \(\omega\) is a bijection’) explicit. It is also the proof shape that generalises: e.g., for any character \(\chi\) of a finite group \(G\) with \(\chi \ne 1\), \(\sum_g \chi(g) = 0\).
  • C is the most visual and easiest to teach. The polygon-closure step is essentially Cavalieri-flavour reasoning; rigorous but visual.

All three give the same result and pairwise reinforce each other. A is most airtight; C is the most pedagogically valuable.

1.6 Verification

  • \(n=4\): \(1 + i + (-1) + (-i) = 0\). ✓
  • \(n=3\): \(1 + e^{2\pi i/3} + e^{4\pi i/3} = 1 + (-\tfrac12 + \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}i) + (-\tfrac12 - \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{2}i) = 0\). ✓
  • \(n=19\) (in-class example): 19 is prime; the 19 vectors are spaced \(\approx 18.95°\) apart, none diametrically opposite, yet sum to 0. The pair-up-by-conjugates trick fails to show this directly (Holmes’s first attempt) — it only proves the imaginary part is 0. Derivation B is the cleanest way to see the real part is also 0.
  • \(n=5\) (regular pentagon): no diametric pairs at all, yet sum = 0 by Derivation C (closed pentagon).

Part II — Order of an element divides \(\varphi(n)\)

2.1 Statement

(Order divides totient). For any \(a\) with \(\gcd(a,n)=1\), \(\text{ord}_n(a) \mid \varphi(n)\). \(\tag{5}\)

2.2 Derivation — Euclidean division contradiction

Let \(r = \text{ord}_n(a)\), so \(a^r \equiv 1 \pmod n\) and \(r\) is minimal. By Euler (Sep 13 AM), \(a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1\). Write \[ \varphi(n) = qr + s, \qquad 0 \le s < r \tag{6} \] (Euclidean division). Then \[ a^{\varphi(n)} = (a^r)^q \cdot a^s \equiv 1^q \cdot a^s = a^s \pmod n. \tag{7} \] Combining with \(a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1\): \[ a^s \equiv 1 \pmod n. \tag{8} \] But \(0\le s < r\) and \(r\) is the smallest positive exponent with \(a^r\equiv 1\). The only consistent value is \(s = 0\), hence \(r \mid \varphi(n)\). \(\quad\blacksquare\)

Weakest step: the minimality of \(r\) in the definition of order is doing the work in step (8). If \(r\) were merely “an exponent that works” rather than the smallest, the conclusion would fail. This is why the proof is by contradiction on \(s\ne 0\).

2.3 Verification on \(n=18\)

\(\varphi(18) = 18\cdot(1-\tfrac12)(1-\tfrac13) = 6\). Compute orders in \((\mathbb Z/18\mathbb Z)^\times = \{1,5,7,11,13,17\}\):

\(a\) \(a^2\) \(a^3\) \(a^6\) \(\text{ord}(a)\)
1 1 1 1 1
5 7 17 1 6
7 13 1 1 3
11 13 17 1 6
13 7 1 1 3
17 1 17 1 2

All orders \(\in \{1,2,3,6\}\) = divisors of 6 = \(\varphi(18)\). ✓

Trick spotted in class: \(5\cdot 7 = 35 \equiv -1 \pmod{18}\). So \(5^3 = ?\): directly \(125 = 6\cdot 18 + 17 \equiv -1\), so \(5^6 \equiv 1\) but \(5^3 \equiv -1 \ne 1\), confirming \(\text{ord}(5)=6\). And \(7^3 \equiv (5\cdot 7)^3 / 5^3 \equiv (-1)^3 / (-1) = 1\), confirming \(\text{ord}(7)=3\). ✓

Definition. \(a\) is a primitive root mod \(n\) iff \(\text{ord}_n(a) = \varphi(n)\) — i.e., it achieves the maximum cycle length. Above: \(5\) and \(11\) are primitive roots mod 18.

Part III — Last three digits of \(7^{2025}\) (homework warmup)

3.1 Setup

Last three digits = mod 1000. \(\varphi(1000) = 1000\cdot(1-\tfrac12)(1-\tfrac15) = 400\). Since \(\gcd(7,1000)=1\), Euler: \(7^{400}\equiv 1\), so \(7^{2025} = 7^{5\cdot 400 + 25} \equiv 7^{25} \pmod{1000}\).

3.2 Derivation A — Binomial \((50-1)^{12}\) in mod 1000

\(7^{25} = 7\cdot 49^{12} = 7\cdot(50-1)^{12}\). Expand keeping only \(50^0\) and \(50^1\) (since \(50^2 = 2500 \equiv 500\), which is not zero mod 1000 — and its coefficient \(\binom{12}{2}=66\) is even, so \(66\cdot 500 = 33000 \equiv 0\), but \(50^3 = 125000 \equiv 0\) for sure):

Lower-order terms only: \[ (50-1)^{12} \equiv (-1)^{12} + \binom{12}{1}\cdot 50\cdot(-1)^{11} + \binom{12}{2}\cdot 50^2\cdot(-1)^{10} \pmod{1000}. \tag{9} \] \[ \equiv 1 - 600 + 66\cdot 2500 = 1 - 600 + 165000 \equiv 1 - 600 + 0 = -599 \equiv 401 \pmod{1000}. \tag{10} \] (Since \(66\cdot 2500 = 165000\), and \(165000 / 1000 = 165\) exactly, so \(\equiv 0\).)

Hence \(7^{25} \equiv 7\cdot 401 = 2807 \equiv 807 \pmod{1000}\).

3.3 Derivation B — CRT into mod 8 + mod 125

\(1000 = 8\cdot 125\), coprime.

Mod 8: \(7\equiv -1\), so \(7^{25} \equiv -1 \equiv 7 \pmod 8\).

Mod 125: \(\varphi(125) = 100\), so \(7^{100}\equiv 1\) but 25 < 100, no reduction. Use binomial: \(7^{25} = 7\cdot(50-1)^{12}\) mod 125. Since \(50^2 = 2500 = 20\cdot 125\), \(50^2\equiv 0\pmod{125}\): \[ (50-1)^{12} \equiv 1 - 600 \equiv 1 - (4\cdot 125 + 100) \equiv -100 \equiv 25 \pmod{125}. \tag{11} \] So \(7^{25} \equiv 7\cdot 25 = 175 \equiv 50 \pmod{125}\).

Wait — let me cross-check (11). \(-600 \pmod{125}\): \(600 = 4\cdot 125 + 100\), so \(-600 \equiv -100 \equiv 25 \pmod{125}\). Then \(1+25 = 26\), so \((50-1)^{12}\equiv 26\), and \(7\cdot 26 = 182 \equiv 57 \pmod{125}\).

(Transcript correction.) The in-class teacher computed \(7\cdot 26 = 182 \equiv 57 \pmod{125}\) — which matches my recomputation. So \(7^{25} \equiv 57 \pmod{125}\), not 50.

CRT recombination. Find \(x\in\{0,\dots,999\}\) with \(x\equiv 57\pmod{125}\) and \(x\equiv 7\pmod 8\).

Candidates \(\{57, 57+250, 57+500, 57+750\} = \{57, 307, 557, 807\}\) (skipping 250 at a time to preserve oddness). Check mod 8: \(57\bmod 8 = 1\); \(307\bmod 8 = 3\); \(557\bmod 8 = 5\); \(807\bmod 8 = 7\). ✓ So \[ 7^{2025} \equiv 807 \pmod{1000}. \tag{12} \] Last three digits: 807. Both A and B agree.

3.4 Sanity by direct cycle

From Sep 13 morning §3.4: \(7^4 \equiv 1\pmod{100}\) (order 4 mod 100). What’s the order mod 1000? Compute: \(7^4 = 2401\); \(7^{20} = (2401)^5\) — too tedious by hand. But from CRT: order mod 1000 = lcm(order mod 8, order mod 125). Order mod 8: \(7\equiv -1\), so order 2. Order mod 125: \(7\) has order dividing \(\varphi(125)=100\); from \(7^{25}\equiv 57\not\equiv 1\), order doesn’t divide 25; checking, the order is 100. So lcm\((2,100)=100\). Thus \(7\) has order 100 mod 1000, and \(7^{2025} = 7^{20\cdot 100 + 25} \equiv 7^{25} \equiv 807 \pmod{1000}\). ✓

Part IV — Counting symmetries of square and cube

4.1 Square: \(|D_4| = 8\)

Definition. A symmetry of a labelled figure is a geometric operation (rotation, reflection, or composition) that preserves the adjacency relationship of vertices.

Direct enumeration. Rotations by \(0°, 90°, 180°, 270°\) (= 4 rotations). Reflections across 4 axes (2 through opposite vertices, 2 through edge midpoints). Total: 8. ✓

Combinatorial count. Place vertex labelled “1” in any of 4 corners (4 choices). Place “2” in either of the two neighbours of “1” (2 choices: clockwise or counterclockwise). Remaining vertices are then forced. Total: \(4\times 2 = 8\). ✓

4.2 Cube: 48 symmetries

Combinatorial count. Place “1” at any of 8 vertices. Place “2” at any of its 3 neighbours. Place “5” (the vertex opposite to one of the remaining two neighbours of vertex 1, chosen as anchor) — 2 choices. Remaining 4 vertices forced. Total: \(8\times 3\times 2 = 48\). ✓

Why direct enumeration is hard. The teacher reports counting \(36\) or \(42\) on first attempts; the missing symmetries are the turn-flips (improper rotations: composition of a rotation with a reflection through the cube’s centre). These are not easily visualised — Wikipedia’s animation is recommended.

Group structure (forward pointer). The 48 symmetries form a group \(\cong S_4 \times \mathbb Z_2\): - \(S_4\) (\(|S_4|=24\)) acts as the rotation group of the cube (= permutation group on the 4 long diagonals). - \(\mathbb Z_2\) is the central inversion (point reflection through centre). - \(24\times 2 = 48\). ✓

Part V — Connection between modular and geometric symmetry

The three threads of this lesson are one structure:

Symmetry source Operation Invariance
\((\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)^\times\), multiplication by \(a\) permutes the unit set \(\prod x_i\) is invariant (Fermat/Euler)
\(n\)-th roots of unity, multiplication by \(\omega\) rotation, permutes the vector set \(\sum \omega^k\) is invariant ⇒ \(=0\)
Square/cube, geometric operations permute the vertex set the figure is invariant

Connection arrow. Derivation B of (1) is the exact geometric analogue of Derivation A of Fermat (Sep 13 morning, eq (2)–(4)). Both invoke: “multiplication by an element permutes a finite set; therefore the sum/product is fixed.” This is the start of the representation theory of finite groups — the unifying language for symmetry in algebra and geometry.

Unified picture

\[\boxed{\text{Finite group } G \xrightarrow{\text{acts on itself}} \text{ permutation } \xrightarrow{\text{sum / product invariant}} \text{numerical identity}}\]

Realisation \(G\) Identity proved
Multiplication mod \(p\) \((\mathbb Z/p\mathbb Z)^\times\) \(a^{p-1}\equiv 1\) (Fermat)
Multiplication mod \(n\) \((\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)^\times\) \(a^{\varphi(n)}\equiv 1\) (Euler)
Rotation of \(\mathbb C\) \(\langle\omega\rangle = \mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z\) \(\sum\omega^k = 0\)
Symmetries of \(n\)-gon \(D_n\) \(|D_n| = 2n\)
Symmetries of cube \(S_4\times\mathbb Z_2\) \(|G|=48\)

Explorative directions

  • Next brick (Sep 20): prove \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\) rigorously — Euler’s formula, used as imported here. The connection \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}\) becomes a theorem, not a definition.
  • Forward (Sep 27): solve \(\cos\theta = a\) for arbitrary (even complex) \(a\) via \(z + z^{-1} = 2a\) — uses the same algebraic toolbox developed here.
  • Deferred from class (homework): (i) prove \(\sum\omega^k = 0\) in your favourite style — the “one-line proof” challenge is Derivation A. (ii) Read AoPS Precalculus Ch 7 (Unit-roots filter) for the application to combinatorial identities, e.g. \(\sum_{k\equiv 0 \pmod n}\binom{N}{k}\).
  • Group-theoretic frame (not in class): the result order divides \(\varphi(n)\) is Lagrange’s theorem restricted to the cyclic subgroup \(\langle a\rangle \subset (\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)^\times\). The general statement: in any finite group \(G\), the order of every element divides \(|G|\).
  • Competition note (teacher’s aside). Teacher explicitly deprioritises AMC/MathCount training: “rigged, time-consuming, doesn’t pay off for college.” Direction is conceptual math, not contest tricks.
  • Open question for later: when is \((\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z)^\times\) cyclic? Answer (not derived in class): exactly when \(n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2p^k\}\) for odd prime \(p\). The non-cyclic cases are exactly where primitive roots fail to exist — e.g. mod 8.
